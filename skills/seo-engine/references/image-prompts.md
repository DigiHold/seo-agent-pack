# Image prompts: the canonical standard for every image

The method here is **identical for every site you run**: only the COLOR / aesthetic differs per site
(bottom). The #1 failure this kills: generic, repetitive images (every post a desk-with-laptop
flat-lay). Every image (featured + one per H2) gets its own exhaustive, topic-specific prompt.

## FULL-BLEED, ZERO MARGIN (HARD GATE, applies to EVERY image)
The subject / UI must **FILL THE ENTIRE FRAME edge to edge, touching all four edges**. **NEVER** a floating
window / card / object centered on an empty studio background with margin around it: NO drop shadow gap, NO
reflection beneath, NO padding, NO border, NO empty negative-space framing. The image must look like a real
full-frame screenshot or photo cropped to 16:9, the subject filling it completely, not a product shot
floating in dead space. The floating-card-with-margin look reads as cheap AI output: reject it. Every
prompt must explicitly say the subject fills the whole frame edge to edge with no margin / no background
around it, and (for UI) "no floating window, no card on a background, no drop shadow, no reflection".

## Method, per image
1. **Pick a REAL composition for the LITERAL subject** (rotate deliberately: never the same twice in a row,
   and the FEATURED must differ in subject from recent articles on that site). Every option shows the
   article's ACTUAL subject, NEVER a symbol or metaphor of it:
   - **The real product / UI / screen**: the actual tool, app, dashboard, website, code editor or interface
     the article is about (UI and short real text ALLOWED when it IS the subject).
   - **Real object in macro**: extreme close-up of the ACTUAL thing the article discusses (the real device,
     part, material, document) with shallow DOF. The real subject, not a stand-in object.
   - **Real environment / scene**: the actual place or setting where the topic happens (a real workshop,
     server room, studio, storefront) with one focal subject + dramatic light.
   - **Real-action close-up**: hands performing the article's ACTUAL task on the real thing.
   - **Cover / document macro**: the real document, card or page the article is about, shot at an angle.
   - **Editorial flat-lay** of the REAL on-topic objects. **Use SPARINGLY: most overused; NEVER the default.**
2. **Map the topic to its LITERAL, DIRECT subject, NEVER symbolism or metaphor.** The image must show the
   REAL thing the article is about, not an analogy for it (e.g. "website maintenance" → a real website / CMS
   dashboard mid-edit, NOT a clock mechanism; "domain name" → a real registrar screen or browser address bar,
   NOT a brass nameplate; "best time to post" → a real scheduling UI or an analytics chart of posting times,
   NOT a sundial). A stranger must see the article's ACTUAL subject in the image. If the subject connects to
   the topic only by analogy, it is BANNED: rewrite it to the literal thing.
3. **Write 500-800 words** covering all 15 elements below, in order. **500 is a HARD FLOOR, no
   exceptions** (250-400-word prompts produce generic, disconnected images that get rejected on sight).
   Count the words; a prompt under 500 = rewrite, not generate.
4. **Cliché check, reject + rewrite if ANY present:** person at desk in front of a laptop; founder
   typing late at night; group around a conference table; smiling person with headphones; generic
   office with a city skyline through windows; stock "businessperson, arms crossed"; the
   desk-flat-lay-with-sticky-notes-and-coffee. **AND, the worst offender for dark/tech brands: abstract
   glowing geometry (floating cubes, orbs, slabs, neon wireframes, particle grids, "energy" beams or
   any generic 3D "tech render").** That reads as cheap AI slop, never as editorial photography. The
   subject MUST be a CONCRETE, real-world, photographable object or scene (a card catalog, a row of vials,
   a printed book, a machinist's press, an architect's model, a brass gauge, etc.) drawn from the concept
   patterns, lit with the brand accent. Real objects, photographed, never abstract shapes that glow.
   **AND reject any METAPHOR / SYMBOLIC STAND-IN**: an object that only represents the topic by analogy
   (hourglass, sundial, padlock-for-vague-security, brass telegraph key, vials, maze, chess piece). Show the
   article's REAL subject instead. The gold standard: for an article on cookie consent compliance, the
   images showed real cookie consent banners, the actual consent-settings UI, the real signal toggles
   in a tag manager, the literal subject every time. Match that.
5. **Topic-clarity check:** would a stranger see the article's ACTUAL subject from the image alone (not a
   symbol of it)? If no, rewrite to the literal subject.

## The 15 mandatory elements (every prompt)
1. **Style declaration**: "Professional editorial photograph, photorealistic documentary style, landscape 16:9".
2. **Subject(s) with exact measurements**: cm/inches, % of frame, spatial relationships.
3. **Material textures at surface level**: grain pores, patina, resin imperfections, fabric weave, metal finish.
4. **Readable text** (only if your site's image policy allows text; the default for SEO images is NO-TEXT, so usually skip): exact quoted short text on a sign/screen/placard.
5. **Camera specs**: exact lens (e.g. 35mm prime), aperture (f/2.0), depth-of-field behaviour (what's sharp, what's soft).
6. **Lighting setup**: sources, angles, colour temps in Kelvin (warm 3200K / cool 5600K), softbox or natural, shadow direction + length.
7. **Colour palette**: named colours + relationships (your site palette below).
8. **Composition**: rule of thirds placement, focal point as % of frame, leading lines, visual weight, negative space.
9. **Background**: depth, what's visible vs faded, atmospheric haze, dust particles.
10. **Atmosphere / mood**: descriptive ("reverent yet eerie", "quiet craftsmanship", "museum-after-hours quiet").
11. **Editorial style references**: Forbes, Wired, HBR, National Geographic, MIT Tech Review, IEEE Spectrum.
12. **Brand accent**: your site's accent colour, integrated naturally (one subtle hint, not plastered).
13. **Topic-specific LITERAL subject**: the REAL thing the article is about, guessable at a glance; never a symbol/metaphor/stand-in.
14. **Strict exclusions**: list what is NOT in frame. ALWAYS exclude: text/letters/numbers, logos, watermarks, readable UI (unless your site's policy explicitly allows text). Add the cliché bans from step 4.
15. **Resolution + sharpness**: "photorealistic high resolution, sharp detail, soft volumetric lighting, dramatic chiaroscuro".

## Your site aesthetic: only the look differs; the method above is identical
Define **ONE fixed brand palette and aesthetic for your site** and reuse it in every image prompt, on
every image, forever. Example of a complete definition: warm near-black background **#141414** + one
muted copper accent **#B87333** (never bright orange), photoreal cinematic still life, filmic color
grade, NO people, NO text. Yours can differ (bright and editorial with real people allowed, clean
natural light, a single brand-colour accent), but decide it ONCE and write it into every prompt.
The COLOURS are the constant; the **CONCEPT must still change every article**: a dark museum-specimen
scene, then a macro detail, then an industrial scene, etc. Never the same scene twice. Also decide once
whether your site allows real people and whether it allows readable text/UI in images, and keep that
decision consistent across the whole site.

## Calibration target
Match the depth of a validated example prompt (a robotic hand encased in amber resin as a "museum
specimen" of a retired AI sales agent: 485 words, all 15 elements). That level of specificity, every
image, every site. A new reader should be able to name the article topic from the picture, and no two
articles on a site should read as "the same photo with different props".
